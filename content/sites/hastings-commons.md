## The place

Hastings Commons is a community-led housing and workspace project in the White Rock area of Hastings, East Sussex. The Observer Building - built in the 1920s, vacated in 1985 when the local newspaper moved out, empty for three decades - is its most visible landmark. Rock House, a nine-storey former office block acquired for £230,000 in 2014, was the beginning. By 2024, Hastings Commons had brought 8,500 square metres of derelict space back to life: 11 rent-capped buildings, 50+ workspaces, 120+ tenants, 700+ CLT members.

The work was started by Jess Steele, via her company Jericho Road Solutions, in partnership with Meanwhile Space CIC. In 2018, when Rock House was valued at £1.6m, they mortgaged it to buy the Observer Building - without knowing where the renovation money would come from. Rents are capped at one third of average local income, permanently. That is what "Hastings Living Rents" means.

## How it works

Hastings Commons Neighbourhood Ventures is owned equally by three shareholders: Jericho Road Solutions, Meanwhile Space CIC, and the Hastings Commons Community Land Trust. The CLT is member-led, democratic, non-profit, jointly owned by over 700 members. The shareholder agreement includes a provision that once buildings reach steady state - covering their own costs - the CLT will hold 100% of the shares. The CLT as a forever organisation, holding assets on behalf of the community in perpetuity.

Residents are selected from the Council's Housing Register using four criteria: housing need, local connection, enthusiasm for the Hastings Commons ethos, and willingness to contribute to the community. All buildings use sustainable design: triple glazing, air source heat pumps, heat recovery, rainwater harvesting.

## What's at stake

Hastings Borough Council is close to Section 114 - the local government equivalent of bankruptcy. There are 1,000 households in temporary accommodation in the town, costing £5m a year - a third of the entire council budget. The gap between Local Housing Allowance and market rent was around £100 in 2015; by 2023 it was nearly £300. In 2024, Hastings Commons won the Gold World Habitat Award for demonstrating "what happens when a community takes charge of its own future."

The question the place poses is structural: in a context of gentrification, market-driven displacement and council financial collapse, what does permanent community ownership actually protect? What does it mean to cap a rent forever? What does it cost, and who pays, and what happens when the one person who made it happen leaves?
