## The place

Granby is four Victorian terrace streets in Toxteth, Liverpool - Beaconsfield, Cairns, Jermyn and Ducie - among the oldest multicultural neighbourhoods in the country. Following the 1981 Toxteth riots and decades of managed decline, the streets were mostly emptied and tinned up. Demolition took the streets around them. These four survived.

For over two decades before any formal organisation existed, residents kept the streets alive - clearing, planting, painting, campaigning. In 2011 they formed the Granby Four Streets CLT. In 2012, urban designers Assemble began working alongside residents on a slow, participatory renovation. In 2015, Assemble won the Turner Prize. The CLT purchased 13 properties on Cairns Street for £1 each, renovated them, and kept going. The work continues.

## How it works

The CLT holds properties and renovates them using low-cost materials and salvage. Granby Workshop - an architectural ceramics workshop set up as part of the Turner Prize nomination - produces objects for sale, with profits reinvested. Some properties are social rent, some affordable sale. The model resists a master plan by design: renovation is incremental, responsive, led by what's there.

## Why I'm going

Because the most interesting question here is not what Assemble did but what the residents were already doing before anyone noticed. Two decades of keeping streets alive without funding, recognition or institutional support. I want to understand that energy - where it came from, what sustained it, what it costs.
