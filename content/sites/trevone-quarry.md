## The place

Trevone Quarry was worked for granite from the early 1800s until the mid-1980s. In 2009, Rob Higgs acquired the 13-acre site outside Penryn. The planning permission at the time was to extract the remaining granite or convert to industrial agriculture. He did neither. Over the following years, the site was planted with eleven acres of broadleaf mixed woodland. Slowly, a community formed.

The site holds four things together at once: a residential community of around twenty people living in self-built structures and caravans; a cluster of creative businesses occupying the former quarry buildings - a blacksmith, a sculptor, a music studio, a theatre set designer, a rum distillery, illustrators, performance artists, writers, painters; a rewilding and species reintroduction project; and a working agro-forest farm. The hope, as Rob puts it, is that these four things benefit each other the way an ecosystem does.

## How it works

What Rob is offering at Trevone is not, as he writes in the residents manual he and Sophie put together, a conventional landlord and tenant arrangement. It is a response - to climate chaos, biodiversity collapse, the cost of living crisis, the mental health crisis, the broken relationship between people and the natural world. The residents manual is one of the more honest documents I have read about what community living actually requires of people.

The model is built around decoupling from the car. No vehicle movements for new residents - not as a punitive rule but as an ecological commitment, and a practical one: every unnecessary vehicle movement risks triggering a planning enforcement notice that would be served not on the individual but on everyone living there. Bicycles, e-bikes, bike trailers, handcarts, wheelbarrows. The nearest bus stop is in Treverva, fifteen minutes' walk from the site.

Residents build their own structures, their own composting toilets, their own showers. Materials are provided. The labour is your own. The philosophy behind this is stated plainly: self-reliance is not an inconvenience to be designed around but a skill to be learned. Quality of life is your own responsibility - and with responsibility comes freedom.

Nature restoration work happens collectively and voluntarily. Rob writes about the awkwardness of paying people for what he sees as something closer to an act of love - damming a stream, clearing invasive species, haloing an oak. He quotes something he half-remembers: *activism is the rent I pay to live on this beautiful planet.* He is not entirely comfortable asking people to do unpaid work, but he is more uncomfortable pretending the discomfort isn't there.

## What's at stake

Nineteen eviction and enforcement notices over the years. Investigations citing slum landlord regulations, fire safety, child welfare. The claim, from neighbours and authorities, that what they were doing was - in the council's own words - "visually objectionable, unsustainable, and doing significant harm to the delicate fabric of the local community." Nearly every investigating officer acknowledged privately that what was happening at Trevone was a good thing. The planning legislation simply wasn't designed to accommodate it. The last battle took four years and cost £80,000 to fight and win.

Rob is clear-eyed about the contradiction he is living inside. He describes himself as operating closer to a benign dictator than to an egalitarian elder. He controls who lives there. He is trying to build a model that inspires other landowners to share their land - but he is also building it on land he owns. He holds these tensions openly rather than resolving them.

## Why this is in the project

Trevone doesn't fit any of the typologies this project works with. It is not a CLT, not a cohousing scheme, not a cooperative in any formal sense. It is a landowner who decided to do something else with his land, without permission and without certainty, and built it over seventeen years. The planning system spent years trying to close it down and couldn't.

I want to understand what that looks like now - and what it means to win.

## From the residents manual

The following is taken from a residents manual compiled by Rob and Sophie Higgs and shared with incoming residents.

---

*"What I am offering here is not a conventional landlord/tenant agreement. Rather a response to the impending climate chaos, biodiversity collapse and the cost of living crisis... I am in a position where I can facilitate those who wish to become part of an answer to all of these current issues, people that want to live on the land but who can't afford to do so in our current system."*

*"It's become a passion for me to inspire people to want to and be able to live a wilder life. To avoid the rat-race of our society with the minimum of bureaucratic headache from our current systems that struggle to allow ways for people to live a life connected to the land."*

*"Whether this place is right for you is a difficult thing to predict or describe, but, in a nutshell, if you want to make the world a better place in some way and feel frustration at the limitations of our modern society and you are happy to swap quite a lot of your creature comforts for a more nature connected life, then it's likely to feel more like home."*

---

The manual ends with two annexes. The first is the prologue to Nick Hayes' *Wild Service: Why Nature Needs You* - on enclosure, the Right to Roam, and the idea that belonging requires active engagement with land in ways that ownership never does. The second is the Galgael Wee Charter, a declaration from a community organisation in Govan, Glasgow - a declaration of independence, interdependence and radical dependence. It ends: *"There is no one coming. There is only us."*

Rob adds a note: he disagrees with that last line. In Cornwall, he thinks, there are loads of lovely people coming together.
