## The place

Trevone Quarry has been worked for granite since the early 1800s. The quarry ceased trading in the mid-1980s. In 2009, Rob Higgs and his partner purchased the 22-acre site outside Penryn in Cornwall. The planning permission at the time was to extract the remaining granite - around a million tonnes still in the ground - or convert to industrial agriculture. They did neither. Over the following fifteen years, they built a woodland, a nature reserve, and a community of around 19 people living in self-built structures and caravans on rewilded land.

Rob describes himself as an apocaloptimist. He believes the days of neoliberal societal organisation are numbered, and that if collapse is even a slight probability, building alternatives now - not from ideology but from love and pragmatism - is simply the sensible response. "Kind of growing a village," he says.

## The idea

What Rob is trying to do at Trevone is not form an intentional community in the conventional sense - no ideology required, no complicated governance, nothing that looks or feels like a cult. The design is deliberate: a model simple enough that other landowners, without being ideologically motivated, might be tempted to share their land with people. The four things the site tries to hold together - residential community, sustainable industrial estate, rewilding project, working farm - are meant to benefit each other the way an ecosystem does. Love and cooperation as the primary driving force. As in nature, he says.

The model for how people live here reflects the same thinking. There is no obligation to work. If you have money but not time, you pay rent and that funds others to do community work. If you have time but not money, five hours of contribution a week and you can live here effectively cash-free. The point is to avoid the conflict that destroys many communities - the expectation of equal labour from people with unequal circumstances. "I want a model to give the freedom to people to do whatever they want to do and follow their dreams, obligations or flow state."

Whatever you build on your own pitch stays with Trevone when you leave. The love put into it remains for whoever comes next.

## The fight

Nineteen eviction and enforcement notices over the years. Investigations using slum landlord regulations, fire safety, child welfare. The claim that what they were doing was "visually objectionable, unsustainable, and doing significant harm to the delicate fabric of the local community." Every investigating officer, Rob notes, acknowledged that what was happening at Trevone was a good thing. The legislation simply wasn't designed to accommodate it. The last planning battle took four years and cost £80,000 to fight and win.

He is direct about his own position: he has subsidised the site from his savings for seventeen years, around £10,000 to £20,000 a year, paid from work and sculpture sales from when he was briefly a well-paid artist. He takes no wage from Trevone. He describes it as a labour of love, a pension of sorts, an inheritance for his family - and as proof of concept. If it can be made to work here, sustainably, without requiring its founder to carry it financially forever, then other landowners might do it too. "So that you guys can go off and start your own communities with lessons of what to do and not to do from this place."

## Why I'm going

Trevone doesn't fit any of the typologies this project works with. It's not a CLT, not a cohousing scheme, not a cooperative in any formal sense. It's something older and harder to categorise - a landowner who looked at the world, decided it was going wrong, and started building something else on his own land, with his own money, over seventeen years, without permission and without certainty. The planning system spent years trying to close it down and couldn't, because what was there was too evidently good to demolish.

I'm also interested in what Rob wrote about cars. He doesn't refuse new residents the right to drive. He simply doesn't offer residency to people who need to drive to get there. The distinction matters to him. It's not a rule - it's a position. "Four wheeled vehicle transport and the fossil fuel industry that supports this is the biggest problem driving the destruction of our natural world. I don't want to facilitate lots more car journeys and thus be part of the problem." Seventeen years in, it works sufficiently, he says.

That quality - making a decision and living it rather than legislating it - is what I want to photograph.
