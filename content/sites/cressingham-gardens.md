## The place

Cressingham Gardens is a council estate of 306 dwellings in Lambeth, south London, designed by borough architect Edward Hollamby and built in the early 1970s. Hollamby's influences ran from the Arts and Crafts movement and William Morris through to Bauhaus - he described his aim as architecture that was "anti-monumental, anti-stylistic and fit for ordinary people." The result is low-rise, pedestrianised, spatially generous: houses and bungalows arranged around a central village green, front doors facing each other across walkways, cars kept to the perimeter, gardens open to the path. A tongue of mature landscape connects the estate to Brockwell Park to the north.

The Rotunda - a circular community building at the heart of the estate - was designed to be the living room of the whole place. Hollamby believed housing was a social art, that the job was not just to build shelter but to build the conditions for community.

## How it works

Cressingham Gardens is a council estate - homes are let at council rent. Residents have no formal ownership stake, but they have organised. The Cressingham Gardens People's Plan, developed in response to Lambeth Council's 2015 demolition proposals, demonstrated that the estate could be refurbished rather than demolished for a fraction of the cost, using the £1.2m annual rental and service charge receipts to fund the work. Architects, residents and campaigners have made the economic and architectural case in detail.

## What's at stake

In 2012, Lambeth Council proposed demolishing the estate and replacing it with apartment blocks. The stated justification was cost - refurbishment estimates were £14,000 higher per dwelling than other estates. Residents have been fighting the decision ever since. In 2016 a High Court ruling found the council had broken the law by removing refurbishment options from consultation. In early 2023, Lambeth paused the redevelopment after a critical report recommended a fundamental reset. The fight is not over.

What is being contested here is not just whether these specific buildings stand or fall, but what a society values when it comes to housing, community and public space. An estate built in the belief that council housing could be dignified, spatial and community-forming now faces demolition on the grounds of cost. The residents who have organised against it are not arguing for heritage preservation; they are arguing that they have the right to stay.
