## The place

Tinkers Bubble is an intentional community near Little Norton in south Somerset, established in 1994 on 40 acres of land - around 20 acres of managed woodland, the rest orchard and pasture. The name comes from the spring that flows through the woodland and ends in a small waterfall by the road; historically, gypsies brought horses here to drink, calling it a bubble. Founded by a group of ten friends including former Ecologist editor Simon Fairlie, the community has outlasted most of what was built alongside it in the 1990s.

The woodland - mostly Douglas fir and larch with patches of native ash - provides fuel for cooking, space-heating and water-heating. A wind generator and solar panels cover electricity. The pasture is maintained with scythes. Two cows, chickens, a horse, an apple press and a vintage steam-powered sawmill. This is not performance or heritage tourism; it is the infrastructure of daily life.

## How it works

The community has planning permission for self-built, temporary structures on the condition that residents make a living from the land. There is a complete ban on fossil fuels on site (paraffin lamps excepted). All decisions are made collectively at monthly meetings; weekly Monday morning meetings handle practical land decisions. The land is held in freehold by a cooperative, Tinker's Bubble Land Ltd.

Income comes from selling sustainably produced timber, apple juice, cider, and organically grown produce at local farmers' markets. Monthly overheads run to around £120 per person. New residents typically enter as volunteers, working the land for extended periods before becoming long-term members - access is through relationship, not money.

## What's at stake

For nearly three decades the community operated under temporary planning permission, renewed periodically, always precarious. In 2023, after 24 years, permanent planning permission was granted - a formal acknowledgement by the state that low-impact, fossil-fuel-free living is sustainable, viable, and worth protecting. The planning battle was not incidental: it was Simon Fairlie's experience here that led him to develop the term Low Impact Development, now a recognised principle in planning law. Many of those who have lived at Tinkers Bubble have gone on to buy land and start their own projects. The community has become, without intending to be, a school.
