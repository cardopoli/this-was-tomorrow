## The place

Homebaked CLT grew from a crisis. In 2002 the neighbourhood around Anfield and Everton was declared a market failure under the Housing Market Renewal Initiative. 1,800 properties were demolished; the promised replacement homes were never built. The scheme collapsed in 2010, leaving the area abandoned. At the centre of it was Mitchell's Bakery - known locally as The Pie Shop, serving pies to locals and football fans since 1903. When the customers were demolished away, the bakery closed.

In 2012, artist Jeanne van Heeswijk and a community cooperative reopened the space as Homebaked Bakery. In 2013, the CLT was formally established. Today the bakery employs 16 people, turns over £500,000 annually, and anchors a broader community-led regeneration of the street. Plans for 26 affordable flats on adjacent land are underway, designed through a participatory process - residents chose the windows, render colours, kitchen and bathroom fittings.

## How it works

The CLT is co-owned by people who live and work in the area. The bakery is a social enterprise - income from trading (pies, ecological dry cleaning, commercial contracts) funds community programmes and subsidises affordable rents. The freehold of the land will pass into community ownership on completion of the housing build. Your Housing is building the homes; Homebaked buys them back.

## Why I'm going

A pie shop as the anchor for a CLT. I'm interested in what it means to use food - something people need every day, something ordinary - as the entry point to something as fraught and complicated as community land ownership. And I want to understand what the neighbourhood actually looks like now, between what was demolished and what's being built.
