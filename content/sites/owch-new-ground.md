  ## The place

  New Ground is the UK's first completed senior cohousing project, built for and by a group of women over 50. The idea began at a 1998 workshop in London, when a small group of women heard a Joseph Rowntree Foundation presentation on Dutch senior cohousing and decided to do the same thing here. The first meeting of what became OWCH - the Older Women's Co-Housing Community - was held in Shirley Meredeen's home in August 1998. The building opened in 2016. It took 18 years.

  New Ground sits on the site of a former convent in High Barnet: 25 self-contained flats, one to three bedrooms, around a shared common house entered through a single front door. One third of the flats are social rent managed by Housing for Women; the rest are leasehold. The 25 residents - women ranging from 50 to 87, mixed wealth and ethnicity - run everything themselves. All decisions are made consensually.

  ## How it works

  OWCH manages the building, organises activities, maintains a pool of potential buyers and renters, and allocates social rental units according to criteria agreed with Housing for Women. Weekly Monday morning meetings for practical decisions; monthly meetings for strategic ones. The model is explicit: privacy where needed, community where valued. Solitude is optional, not inevitable.

  The funding came from multiple sources - Housing Corporation grant, Tudor Trust capital grant and running costs, Hanover Housing Association (who own the site), architects Pollard Thomas Edwards. No single funder made it possible; no single funder controls it now. The planning decision in 2013 was won partly on the argument that senior cohousing reduces demand on health and social care services.

  ## What's at stake

  That it took 18 years to build tells you something about the structural barriers to community-led housing for older people in the UK. Many sites were explored and lost. Funding streams appeared and disappeared. The model was unfamiliar to every institution the group approached. What kept it alive was the group itself - women who continued to organise, advocate and wait, over nearly two decades.

  Shirley Meredeen, 84 when the building opened and one of only two founding members to see it realised, said: "It's never been done before, and we will be completely running it ourselves. We are making history, and we are extremely proud. We are not going to be a little ghetto of older people, we want to be good neighbours."
